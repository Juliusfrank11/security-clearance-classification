In Applicant’s answer to the SOR, she admitted the delinquent obligations set forth in the SOR and indicated she was establishing repayment plans with her creditors. After a thorough review of the pleadings, exhibits, and transcript, I make the following findings of fact. Applicant is a 45-year-old over-the-road truck driver who has worked for a defense contractor  since  May  2017  and  seeks  to  obtain  a  security  clearance.  Her  income  is dependent on the number of loads she hauls.  Her income last year was between $35,000  and  $40,000,  which  included  $628  monthly  from  her  ex-husband’s  military retirement.  From August 1993 thought September 1994, she honorably served in the U.S. Air Force.  In 1993, she had been granted a secret security clearance.  She  left  the  Air  Force  in  1994  due  to  her  pregnancy.    She  has  two daughters  ages  24  and  21.    In  April  2010,  she  was  divorced  having separated from her ex-husband in 2008.  Applicant’s current partner is also an over-the-road truck driver who holds a DoD security  clearance.    Applicant  and  her  partner  both  have  their  Transportation Security Administration  clearance.  Applicant and her partner hope to save $30,000 over the next five years in order to purchase their own truck.  Following her divorce, Applicant lived with a girlfriend who caused serious financial problems. Applicant was the only one working and had difficulty controlling her friend’s spending.  Applicant was an assistant store manager making ten dollars per hour.  In September 2013, the relationship with the friend ended.  From April 2014 through January 2016, Applicant was unemployed.  She has lived with her cohabitant since January 2014.  On  Applicant’s  September  2017  Electronic  Questionnaires  for  Investigations Processing , she indicated she had filed for Chapter 7 Bankruptcy protection in 2012.  She had been unemployed from March 2011 through October 2011. 2  The bankruptcy listed assets of $40,585 and liabilities of $78,884.  A mobile home/trailer was surrendered during the bankruptcy and her debt was discharged  in  the  bankruptcy  proceedings.    She  did  not  list  any  financial delinquencies on routine accounts on her e-QIP, other than the bankruptcy. The SOR lists 13 past due, charged-off, or collection accounts totaling $33,483. Four of the debts totaling $14,147 were for student loans.  Applicant’s student loan obligations  represents  42  percent  of  her  total  delinquent  obligations.  Applicant  has contacted 11 of the 13 creditors and set up payment arrangements or, in some cases, has paid off the debts.  She intends to pay all of her delinquent accounts.  She contacted the creditor for a $609 medical debt  and was told the account could no longer be found.  She is continuing to pursue this obligation.  The other creditor she has been unable to reach was the holder of the vehicle debt .  In  Applicant’s  April  2018  Enhanced  Subject  Interview,  she  indicated  she  was unemployed from March 2014 through January 2016.  Following her divorce, she lived  with  a  girlfriend  who  contributed  to  Applicant’s  financial  problems  leading  to  the bankruptcy.  Applicant’s  credit  score  was  almost  800  before  this  girlfriend  caused  her financial difficulties.  Applicant had two car payments, but could not afford to pay both  of  them  after  she  became  unemployed.  In  late  2012  or  early  2013,  she  had purchased a $17,000 vehicle.  In 2015, one vehicle was voluntarily repossessed resulting in a $16,536 debt  being charged off in September 2015.  As of June 2018, the balance on the debt was $14,165.  It took the creditor over a year to repossess the car after being told to come and get it.  She is continuing in her efforts to reach the creditor.  The  $549  credit  card  collection  account    was  a  credit  card  account opened after the bankruptcy.  Applicant entered into a repayment with the collection agency to make $25 monthly payments on the debt.  She has made her required payments from March 2019 through September 2019.  Applicant attended a university for four semesters in 2015 and 2016. She obtained four student loans, one each for the four semesters. Two of the loans  have been placed for collection.  Her other two  loans  were  $322    and  $328   are 120 days or more past due.  In November 2018, she entered  into  a  rehabilitation  plan  with  the  U.S.  Department  of  Education  whereby  she would make $5 monthly payments, an amount calculated on her income.  From November 2018 through September 2019, she made her monthly payments as required.  She has requested to continue payments by automatic withdrawal  from  her  bank  account  starting  in  November  2019,  which  will  put  her  on  a regular payment plan.  Applicant intends to give priority to resolving her other debts before significantly increasing her student loan payments.  3 Following  her  divorce,  Applicant  did  not  have  health  insurance  and  when  she obtained a job, her health insurance did not cover the entire cost of medical treatment. She underwent gall bladder surgery and had a separate treatment for a broken foot.  She has two medical collection accounts of $618  and $609 . She also is delinquent on a third medical account of $469 .  She has a repayment agreement on the third account whereby she pays $78 monthly since March 2019.  In October 2018, Applicant entered into a repayment agreement with the holder of the $618 medical debt to pay $51.56 monthly on the debt.  She honored the agreement and paid off the debt in August 2019.  The $249 charged-off account  for a utility bill was paid in full in March 2019.  The $802 collection account  was a credit card account.  In November 2018, the creditor offered to settle the debt for $401, which Applicant accepted and paid.  Applicant owed a collection agency $1,468  for a telecommunications debt.  In March 2019, she made a repayment agreement with the creditor to pay $118 monthly for 11 months.  Starting in March 2019, she made her monthly payments on the debt.  As of August 2019, she owed only one more payment on a balance of $118.  A collection agency was attempting to collect $407  for a credit card.  In March 2019, she entered into a repayment agreement with the creditor to pay $89 monthly for three months.  In April 2019, May 2019 and June 2019, she honored the repayment agreement by making the $89 monthly payments on the debt.  Applicant is current on her $450 monthly rent, her $392 monthly  loan payments on her 2008 vehicle, and the $628 monthly payments on a 2011 motorcycle.  She can afford her monthly obligations.
