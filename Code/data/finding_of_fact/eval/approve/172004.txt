After   a   thorough   and   careful  review   of  the   pleadings  and   exhibits submitted, I make the  following additional findings of fact. Applicant  is  41  years  old.  He  and  his  wife  separated  in  April  2015.  She  is  a citizen and resident of a European country. He has no children.  He earned a bachelor’s degree while in the U.S. Air Force and took some later courses but has not earned a further degree.  Applicant served in the Air Force from 2004-2011 as an intelligence analyst, with a Top Secret/SCI clearance. He was discharged honorably as an E-5.  After leaving the Air Force in December 2011, he moved to Europe and attempted to gain employment as a U.S government contractor.  He  worked  for  about  six  months  at  a  mall  on  a  U.S.  military  base  there  .  He  was  then  unemployed  for  the  rest  of  2012,  after  returning  to  the United  States.    He  worked  for  several  months  in  2012  as  a  sales representative  for  a  cereal  company,  before  being  laid  off  in  July  2013.  He  was  then unemployed for about two months.  Applicant then worked for a defense contractor as a data analyst from September 2013 to September 2015, before he was laid off. He was then unemployed for most of the next two years, until June 2017.  From then until beginning his current job, he worked several federal contractor jobs, interspersed with periods of unemployment.  His prior position, with a federal contractor, was  under  a  six-month  contract  ,  at  the  equivalent  of $90,000 annually.  Applicant  has  worked  for his current  employer since  February 2020.  He  has  an annual salary of $92,400.  He is a data analyst working with  veterans’  healthcare data.  He also receives $1,500 in veteran’s disability insurance due to a 70% service-connected disability rating, including for post-traumatic stress disorder .  Applicant is in related counseling.  Applicant’s mother died tragically in a house fire in September 2013. He also had difficulty  dealing  with  his  marital  separation  in  2015,  which  led  to  depression  and anxiety.  He  acknowledged,  however,  that  his  debts  are  largely  due  to  his  own mismanagement and financial irresponsibility, though it was during “a very dark, difficult period in [his] life.”  The delinquent debts alleged in the SOR include about $49,500 in student loans ; credit-card debts of about $22,000 ,  along  with  smaller  phone    and  medical  debts .  Applicant  denied  all  the  debts  in  the SOR  but  clarified  at  his  hearing  that  he  acknowledges  responsibility  for  them  though many are now paid.  The debts are established by the credit reports in the record.  Applicant testified  that once  his employment stabilized, he  contacted  the student loan  creditors to  renew  payments in about September 2019. Between  then  and  March 2020, Applicant made  six  or seven  monthly  payments of  $416  to  rehabilitate  his  student loans.   They  are all  now  considered  rehabilitated  and  no  longer delinquent.    A   January   2022   credit  report  shows  that  Applicant’s student  loans  are  in   “pays  as  agreed”   status. He  is making  payments on  his private   loans   and  his federal   loans are  in   COVID-related  forbearance.
