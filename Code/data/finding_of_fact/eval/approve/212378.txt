Applicant’s then-husband lost his job  in State  2  in about September 2019.  They were  offered  jobs at military facilities in State  3. In  September 2019, Applicant moved  to  State  3  for a  job,  but  he  did not  move  from  State  2. He  told her he  wanted  a divorce  in May 2021.  Applicant worked in City 1, in State 3, for two years, until September 2021, when she moved to her current location in the same state.  She worked for contractor A in State 3 until the Friday before the hearing. She worked full time and earned $34.79 an hour. On the day of the hearing, as noted above, she began a new job on the same base, with a new employer. She received a $7 an hour pay raise.  As  amended, the SOR concerns 11  alleged  delinquent debts,  discussed below. They include federal and state income tax debts, consumer debts, a repossession, past- due rent, and other debts. The debts are established by credit reports  from November 2022  and  April  2023,  and  other  record  evidence.  .  In  her  Answer  to  the original  SOR,  Applicant  admitted  all  of  the  debts,  and  said  they  were  being  resolved through bankruptcy  or were the responsibility of her ex-husband. 3 The debts alleged are due to Applicant’s ex-husband’s employment issues and his refusal to address the marital debts. They also were not able to afford to live in State 1, a state with high taxes and a high cost of living. They addressed that by moving to State 2. Applicant did not have steady work until she moved to State 3.  SOR ¶ 1.a  concerns past-due federal income taxes, for tax years  2014, 2017, 2020, and 2021.  Most of these debts were incurred when Applicant and her then-husband were living in State 1. They filed joint income tax returns before their divorce. They were making payments, but he stopped doing so. They found they could not afford to remain in State 1, so they moved to State 2. Her then-husband refused to pay the taxes or any of their marital debt. “He just didn’t care,” she said. They could not keep up with the tax burden, and always owed $2,000 or $3,000  each year. State 2, where they moved, does not have a  state  income  tax.  Applicant  has filed her federal income taxes individually since the divorce.  Under their divorce decree, Applicant’s then-husband  was ordered to pay “All IRS past-due taxes.”  Even so, Applicant included the tax debts in her bankruptcy per her counsel’s advice.  SOR ¶ 1.b  concerns past-due state income taxes owed to State 3 for TY 2020.  Applicant  was assigned responsibility for any State 3 past-due taxes in the divorce decree, along with any debts incurred after their May 29, 2021 separation date.  Her State 3 tax debt is included in the bankruptcy.  SOR ¶ 1.l is a 2020 state tax lien, for $3,309  in past-due state income tax debt owed to State 1.  Applicant initially denied the debt because she was not aware of it.  She then researched the matter diligently in the days before the hearing and learned the debt was valid. She plans to include this tax debt in he pending Chapter 13 bankruptcy proceeding.   The following SOR debts are all listed on credit reports in the record as past due and in collection status:  SOR ¶ 1.c , to a communications company; SOR ¶ 1.d , to an insurance company; SOR ¶ 1.e , to a lending company; SOR ¶ 1.f , a gas credit card; SOR ¶ 1.h , for past-due rent; and SOR ¶¶ 1.i  and 1.j , which are consumer accounts.  SOR ¶ 1.g  is the balance due on a repossessed auto.  This car was repossessed after Applicant’s then-husband lost his job in State 1. They could only keep one vehicle so they returned this one.
