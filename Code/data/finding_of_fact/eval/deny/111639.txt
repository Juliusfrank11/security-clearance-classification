Applicant  is  a  48-year-old  protective  security  officer  who  has  served  in  that profession  for  different  entities  since  2004.  She  has  completed  nearly  100  college credits.  Twice  divorced  and  remarried,  she  has  four  children  in  their  20s.  At  issue  are 18  federal  and  state-related  tax  issues  ,  including failure  to  timely  file  multiple  federal  and  state  tax  returns,  and  three  delinquent commercial accounts . In sum, nearly $70,000 of delinquent taxes and  delinquent  debts  are  at  issue,  with  almost  $58,000 related to  taxes  and  the balances related to a medical account , a charged off credit card , and about $4,774 past due on a student loan with a total balance of $86,210. In  2004,  Applicant  divorced  her  second  husband.  The  resultant  lack  of  housing and financial distress led her to a three-month period of homelessness  while taking care of her children.  Related financial problems lasted  until  at  least  2009,  when  some  of  her  children  went  to  live  elsewhere  and  she was obligated to pay child support. Meanwhile, she failed to timely file federal and state tax returns. Applicant’s main focus had been “trying to survive,” caring for her children, maintaining long hours associated with her work, and, occasionally, taking second jobs.  Applicant did not know she could seek an extension to file her taxes.  As  a  result,  “personal  responsibility  to  take  care  of  [her]  administrative  paperwork  .  .  . fell  by  the  wayside.”    She  noted  that  “during  [her]  challenges,  unfortunately, paying the taxes became secondary to initial survival and recovery.”   By 2013, she was confused as to her financial situation in terms of taxes.  As before, “other  things  were  happening  in  [her]  life  and  [she]  didn’t  stay  on  top  of  it.”   Meanwhile,  errors  with  her  taxes  and  tax  forms  were  encountered,  such  as  lost  or missing  W-2  tax  statements  and  delays  from  employers  to  get  copies  of  needed paperwork.  In  sum,  federal  tax  returns  were  not  timely  filed  for  tax  years    2004-2006, 2008-2015,  and  2017  ; state tax returns were not timely filed for TYs 2004, 2008- 2009, and 2012-2013 ; delinquent state taxes owed to a second state for about $1,300); and a state tax return was not timely filed to a third state for TY 2017 . Applicant testified that her tax returns for TY 2017 and 2018 were filed in April 2019.  As  for  her  federal  tax  liability  of  around  $52,000,  Applicant  initially  made  some payments  to  the  Internal  Revenue  Service  .    Those  proceeds,  however, were diverted to her ex-husband by the IRS to address her child support arrearage.   In  2013,  a  tax  advisor  briefly  started  reviewing  Applicant’s  federal  tax  situation before  the  job  went  to  someone  else.    Tax  transcripts  suggest  zero  dollars are currently owed for TY 2004, 2005, 2006, 2012, and 2015 due to methods such as application  of  refunds,  but  Applicant’s  information  indicate  confusion  remains.   After the SOR was issued in September 2019, Applicant’s present husband suggested she revisit a tax attorney and let him attempt to negotiate and compromise with the IRS on  the  amount  owed.    The  attorney  initiated  that  action  in September 2019, shortly before the hearing.  At present, the attorney is trying to get her federal tax situation contained and resolved.  That  attorney  is  also  working  with  Applicant’s  state  tax  issues,  a  job  he  initially started in 2013 before personal “life issues” arose that diverted him from her case.   Applicant’s  tax  liability  to  her  primary  state,  the  approximately  $5,000  at  issue, was initially reviewed by the tax advisor. Actual progress on that state’s taxes, however, was successfully initiated and made by that state in and after 2013. The state’s efforts brought Applicant’s tax balance owed to that state to zero through garnishment and tax refunds. . The  attorney  is  also  working  on  her  state  tax  issues  with  a  second  state.  The second  state’s  tax  liability  was  inadvertently  satisfied  when  payments  to  a  third  state were misapplied by Applicant’s employer .   They  were  for  delinquent  taxes  dating  back  to  2014  and  2015.    This followed Applicant’s initiation of an installment repayment plan in June 2019, reflected at Ex.  H.    She  testified  that  she  also  had  been  making  regular  payments  toward those  balances  through  her  online  bank  account,  but  she  provided  no  documentation reflecting  such  payments.    She  also  testified  that  the  state  at  issue  has  no records of her employer paying certain taxes to it on her behalf.  The attorney is also handling the tax issue involving a third state. For that state, Applicant testified that a belated tax return was filed, but she is awaiting a refund from another state to satisfy the balance owed.  With  regard  to  a  student  loan  account  noted  at  SOR  allegation  ¶  1.r,  Applicant showed that she consolidated her student loans in May 2019.  She was approved in October 2019, the day before the hearing, to be in forbearance on the account  through  May  2020  in  order  to  work  on  her  tax  issues.   Applicant testified she has been making payments on a charged off credit card, noted at SOR allegation ¶ 1.s for $2,437, but provided no documentation of such payments after the hearing.  A medical debt, noted at SOR allegation ¶ 1.t from 2016 for $2,117 has been satisfied.  3 Applicant  and  her  husband  own  their  home  on  which  they  pay  a  mortgage. Applicant’s  husband  fully  supports  his  wife  in  addressing  her debts and is willing to add his financial resources toward her effort to satisfy her debts when matters are sorted and balances are clarified. [She] was so busy taking care of others that  [she]  neglected  [herself]  and  [her]  responsibility.  .  .  .”    At  present,  her finances are stable and her life is peaceful.
