In Applicant’s SOR response, she admitted the SOR allegation in ¶ 1.a, and she denied  the  allegations  in  SOR  ¶¶  1.b  and  1.c.    She  also  provided  mitigating information. Her admissions are accepted as findings of fact. Applicant  is  50  years  old,  and  she  has  been  an  acquisitions  lead  and  senior program analyst for a DOD contractor for five years.  She has been employed by the same employer or a predecessor employer of her current employer since 1996.  In 1988, she graduated from high school.  She completed three years of college, and she has not received a degree.  She has not served in the military.  She was married from 1987 to 1989, from 1992 to 2004, and from 2004 to 2008.  Her children are ages 20, 25, and 31.  She has custody of a four-year-old  child.    She  has  held  a  secret  security  clearance  since  2008,  and there is no evidence of security violations.  Financial Considerations Applicant was not unemployed or under employed in the previous 10 years. She did not disclose any circumstances beyond her control that prevented her from paying her  debts.  She  works  one  full-time  and  one  part-time  job  to  maintain  her  home  and vehicle.  For the last several years, she has received $3,000 to $4,000 each year from  her part-time employment.   She actively  supports a  high  school and a local youth football organization.  The SOR alleges three delinquent debts totaling $33,645 as follows: SOR  ¶  1.a  alleges  that  Applicant  has  a  debt  placed  for  collection  for  $33,001. About 10 years ago, Applicant borrowed about $35,000 using a line of credit option on a 2 credit card to renovate her basement after it flooded.  She made payments for a time, and then the creditor increased her interest rate without notifying Applicant.  The interest rate went from 4.9 percent to 27.9 percent.  She denied  that  she  missed  a  payment.    The  creditor  was  unwilling  to  accept  a payment for the amount of her previous payments.  The creditor demanded a $900 monthly payment and would not accept a  $400 payment.  The balance was about $25,000 in August 2013.  In November 2015, a collection agent took over the account.  She did not receive correspondence from the collection agent, and she did not contact the collection agent.  In October 2019, Applicant called the original creditor, and the original creditor told her the account is closed because of the age of the debt.  Moreover, the creditor did not have a record of the account.  She acknowledged she was responsible for the debt; she was willing to pay it; and she simply did not know who to pay or how to pay it.  After  the  hearing,  she  contacted  the  collection  agent,  and  on  January  27,  2020,  the collection agent agreed to settle the debt for $8,500.  On January 27, 2020, she paid the creditor $2,550, and on January 29, 2020, she paid the creditor $6,000.  This debt is settled and resolved. SOR ¶¶ 1.b and 1.c allege that Applicant has two delinquent medical accounts for $251 and $393. Applicant said she paid both debts in 2018.  On January 22, 2020, the creditor for the debt in SOR ¶ 1.b wrote that on October 30, 2018, the debt was “paid in full.”  On February 11, 2020, the creditor in SOR ¶ 1.c wrote that on October 30, 2018, the creditor received a payment for $393, and the debt was “PAID IN FULL.”  Applicant’s annual salary is $94,000.  She files her tax returns.  She maintains $1,000 to $3,000 in her bank accounts.  Her monthly mortgage payment is $1,149.  One of her vehicles does not have a lien, and she pays $694 monthly for the other vehicle.  She has a net monthly remainder after paying debts and expenses of about $500.  Applicant’s May 10, 2019, and January 20, 2020 credit reports indicated only one negative  entry,  and  that  negative  entry  relates  to  SOR  ¶  1.a.   Numerous accounts reflected a zero balance, paid, or otherwise successfully resolved. Her January 20, 2020 credit report shows good credit scores.
