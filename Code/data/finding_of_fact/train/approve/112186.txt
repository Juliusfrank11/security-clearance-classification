Applicant  is  41  years  old.  He  was  married  in  2003  and  separated  in  2014.  His divorce  became  final  in  June  2017.    Applicant  and  his  ex-wife  raised  five children. The oldest three are adults, in the service or college. Applicant and his ex-wife share  custody  of  the  youngest  two  boys,  ages  12  and  14.  He  obtained  a  bachelor’s degree  in  2010,  and  reports  no  military  service.    Applicant  has  been employed by a federal contractor as a consultant since November 2011. Applicant’s ex- wife did not work outside the home. The SOR alleged a debt to the federal government for past-due [sales] taxes in the amount of $115,861  and a debt to state A for past-due-income taxes in the amount of $10,486 . It also alleged a charged-off debt for a credit card in  the  amount  of  $62,147  .  Applicant  admitted  all  of  the  allegations  in  his Answer to the SOR. Applicant testified credibly that he initially had tax problems when he changed status from a W-2 to a 1099 employee in 2009-2010.  He didn’t understand  that  he  was  supposed  to  estimate  and  pay  quarterly  taxes.  He  had  a certified  public  accountant    prepare  and  file  returns  at  the  end  of  each  tax  year . However, he could not afford to pay the taxes due. So, he entered into a payment plan and has paid all income taxes owed for TY 2009-2010, which are not alleged in the SOR. Applicant  was  working  full  time  as  a  federal  contractor  in  2013.  He  decided  to start a retail store to supplement his income.  He provided the lease for Chatman Solutions,  LLC,  which  required  that  he  make  payments  of  $5,500  over  five  years starting  on  November  1,  2013.    This  was  his  first  venture  into  retail.  Almost immediately,  a  large  Walmart  store  opened  up  across  the  street  from  his  store, siphoning off business.  Applicant struggled and didn’t even break even on the store.  He slept on a couch in the back of the store as he separated from his wife in 2014. Applicant’s  IRS  tax  transcripts  and  W-2’s  were  attached  to  his  answers  to interrogatories.  They reveal his adjusted gross income  of $247,000 in TY 2012; $158,000 in TY 2013; $143,000 in TY 2014; $160,000 in TY 2015; and $124,000 in  TY  2016.  He  testified  that  he  filed  federal  and  state  income  tax  returns  timely  each year, but he could not afford to pay the taxes owed due to his alimony and child support payments, failing store, and divorce.  He admits to also making some bad decisions about money.  He contemplated filing for bankruptcy protection, but considered that to be a cop out, and avoided it.  Applicant testified about an earlier installment payment plan that he had with the IRS in 2013 for overdue taxes from TY 2011. He detrimentally relied on a CPA who led Applicant  to  believe  this  was  in  a  non-collectible  status.    His  repayment agreement with the IRS fell apart after 18 months when Applicant encountered financial 3 distress  with  his  retail  store,  and  became  separated  in  2014.    On  March  21, 2018, Applicant entered into an Offer in Compromise with the IRS after providing all of his financial information to the IRS.  The IRS used a formula to set the amount of  the  payment  due  and  he  settled  for  $8,000,  conditional  upon  his  timely  filing  and paying all taxes due for five years.  An October 7, 2018, letter from the IRS to Applicant accepted his offer in compromise and included a copy of a check to IRS  from  Applicant  for  $6,575.  This  was  the  balance  owed  for  TY  2012,  according  to IRS tax transcripts.  The tax transcripts reflect a zero balance owed for TYs 2013, 2014, 2015, and a $15,185 balance for TY 2016.  Applicant testified credibly that all of his overdue federal taxes have now been paid off.  Applicant  provided  evidence  of  financial  counseling  from  his  aunts  and  a  well- known company with an action plan to formulate a budget and payment plan.  He also provided 16 character reference letters, all attesting to his positive attitude, integrity,  work  ethic,  trustworthiness  and  reliability.
