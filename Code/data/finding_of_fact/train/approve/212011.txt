Applicant is a 57-year-old woman with three adult children. Although she is legally married, she has been separated from her husband since September 2021, and they are in the process of filing for a divorce.  Applicant  is  a   high   school  graduate   and   she   earned   an   associate   degree   in business   administration   in  1999.    Currently,  she   works  for  a   contractor  as  a security specialist.  She  has been  promoted  twice during  the  past six months.  She has held a security clearance since 2014.   Since   2002,  financial  problems  have   compelled   Applicant  to   file  for  bankruptcy protection    three    times.    Applicant’s  first  bout  with    financial  difficulties,  beginning    in  the early 2000s,  occurred  when  her husband,  a  U.S. military member,  received  permanent change  of station orders to  a  location  where  expenses such as housing  and  DOD  public schooling  were  not provided, and there  was no  convenient access to  a  commissary.   Applicant, then  a  stay-at-home  mother, considered  taking  a  job  to  help  make  ends meet.   However, this proved  untenable  as she  was pregnant with  her third  child and  was pre-occupied  with  raising  the  two  older children.  Subsequently, Applicant and  her  husband  decided  to  file  for Chapter 7  bankruptcy protection.   On  May 7, 2002, the  bankruptcy judge  discharged  their  debt. The  amount of the  discharged  debt is unknown from the record. Applicant’s next bout of financial problems occurred approximately five years later and was triggered  by marital problems when  her husband  “abandoned  [Applicant] and [their] kids”  and began seeing another woman.   When her husband moved, he stopped helping pay the bills. Furthermore, Applicant discovered that he had not been using  the  base  housing  allowance  to  pay  the  rent,  and  that  they  were  three  months behind.  Although Applicant was working at this time, she again fell behind on the bills.  2 Applicant filed for Chapter 13 bankruptcy protection in 2007.  She included $45,000  of  debt  in  the  bankruptcy  petition.    Applicant  failed  to  make bankruptcy payments, prompting the court to dismiss the petition in July 2008.  Subsequently, Applicant and her husband reconciled.  At or about this time, Applicant’s financial fortunes changed for the better, as she obtained a job as a general manager of a major restaurant chain, and a part-time job working at a hospital.  The income from these jobs, together with the income of her husband, enabled Applicant to satisfy her delinquent debts. For  the  next  ten  years  Applicant’s  finances  remained  stable.    Then,  in approximately 2018, her husband lost his job.  Depressed, he struggled with suicidal ideation, and stopped either looking for another job or helping pay the bills.  Their joint income decreased by $250,000, to $50,000 annually.  In September 2019, Applicant and her husband  filed for Chapter 13 bankruptcy.  The petition included $97,291 of debt.  Applicant’s financial situation worsened further when her employer laid her off from the job the same month.  Shortly thereafter, the bankruptcy court dismissed the bankruptcy petition.  Applicant remained unemployed for four months.  Applicant was  unemployed  through  February  2020.   In  2021,  she  and her husband  again  separated.   In  September 2022, they sold their  house  for 1.1 million  dollars.   They had  originally purchased  it in 2016  for $800,000.  They netted  $140,000  which  they split. The  SOR alleges  that Applicant did  not disclose  her debts  that had  been  either turned  over to  collection, suspended,  charged  off,  or  cancelled  in  the  past  seven  years, as required,  on  a security clearance application   completed in February 2020.
