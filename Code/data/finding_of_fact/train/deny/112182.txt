He served in the enlisted ranks in the National Guard from February 1990 to February 1993, while also working as a Federal employee from July 1990 to December 1994.  He held a top secret clearance and sensitive compartmented information  eligibility from August 1990 to late November 2014.  Applicant has worked for his current defense-contractor employer since August 2018.  He was previously employed by the company from December 1995  to  June  2012.  According  to  his  former  supervisor,  Applicant  held  his  security clearance without any issue for the duration of his tenure at the company.  3 Applicant married in November 1998. He and his now ex-wife had two sons, who are 16 and 13.  He and his ex-wife began having marital problems in 2008.  In late July 2010, Applicant began treatment with a licensed professional counselor also licensed in marriage and family therapy.  He and his spouse attended two or three sessions as a couple, but his ex-wife was not interested in pursuing marital counseling.  Applicant and his ex-wife separated in July 2011.  Applicant moved into an apartment, and his ex-wife continued to reside in the marital residence with their  two  sons  for  another  year.    In  January  2012,  on  his  attorney’s  advice, Applicant began paying spousal and child support totaling $5,007 per month  on his gross income of $12,933 per month.  Applicant defaulted on a credit-card account  after July 2011, and the creditor eventually charged off his account for $34,264.  Applicant could not maintain his support obligations and living expenses if he paid the credit card.  In approximately February 2012, Applicant’s then employer laid off some managerial staff. Applicant took a reduction in salary of $25,000 so that he could stay on for a few months until June 2012.  Applicant began a new job almost immediately as a senior principal  systems  engineer  with  another  defense  contractor.    On annual wages totaling $129,498 in 2012, he paid $44,705 in alimony.  Applicant and his ex-wife were divorced in February 2013. On his monthly gross salary of $12,833, Applicant agreed to pay spousal support for his wife for seven years from February 2013, initially at $3,400 per month and then declining by $200 each year thereafter, subject to recalculation should his earnings decline by 10% or more from a stipulated $148,000 in gross income and his ex-wife’s earnings equal or exceed $40,000 annually.  Applicant  also  agreed  to  pay  child  support  of  $1,174  per  month  starting  in February 2013 for his sons, to terminate when his sons turn 18 or graduate from high school or are not self-supporting and not yet 19 years old.  In their divorce settlement, Applicant and his ex-wife were held fully responsible for their own debts. At the time of their divorce, Applicant and his ex-wife jointly owned their marital residence, which was encumbered by a deed of trust securing payment of a joint note  in the approximate amount of $449,000 . Neither Applicant nor his ex-wife made the payments on the mortgage or the HELOC after July 2012. Applicant could not afford to make the payments.  His rent was $1,850 per month , and he had incurred $20,000 to $30,000 in legal fees for his divorce.  A buyer was willing to purchase the home in a short sale, but the HELOC remained in collections, and they lost the buyer. In April 2013, the primary lender foreclosed on the property.
