Applicant is 52 years old. He was married from 1990 to 2017.  His wife is deceased. He has two adult children that live with him.  Applicant obtained his Bachelor’s  degree  in  electrical  engineering  in  1988.    He  reports  no  military service.  He  has  been  employed  as  an  engineer  for  a  major  federal  contractor  for  22 years.  He received exemplary performance evaluations. He provided a resume and seven pages of individual achievement awards and certificates.  He reports having a security clearance since 1997 with no incidents.  The SOR alleges four delinquent debts totaling $394,812 including a charged-off mortgage  loan  at  SOR  ¶  1.a,  in  the  amount  of  $239,347,  and  a  charged-off  second mortgage  on  the  same  property  at  SOR  ¶  1.b.  It  also  alleges  a  related  debt  in  the amount  of  $69,648  placed  for  collection  at  SOR  ¶  1.d.  Applicant  co-invested  in  two homes  with  others  in  2006,  and  he  purchased  a  third  home  himself  that  year,  at  the height of the housing market bubble.  This was an investment strategy launched when  the  properties  were  appreciating  in  value.  Almost  immediately,  the  market crashed  with  the  subprime  mortgage  scandal  of  2008  and  ensuing  financial  collapse. Applicant  and  his  co-investor  sold  the  first  house  and  he  incurred  a  loss  of  a  few hundred  dollars.    They  sold  the  second  house  around  2009  and  he  incurred  a loss of approximately $15,000.  Applicant owned house number three  alone. It was losing value rapidly. He tried  a  short  sale  of  the  property  but  was  informed  that  he  would have  to  stop  making payments. He did so, but the lender would not agree to the short sale anyway.  In  2012,  Applicant  was  struggling  to  make  mortgage  payments  because  his  overtime opportunities decreased. He obtained a home-equity line of credit  of $98,700 to use for mortgage payments on house # 3.  He knew it was depreciating but  hoped  that  the  market  would  recover.    He  tried  to  sell  it  but  received  no offers.  Eventually,  he  gave  up  and  both  the  primary  and  second  loan  were  defaulted. He  allowed  it  to  go  to  a  foreclosure-auction  sale  in  February  2014.    Applicant had the two Bank of America  mortgage loans on the property totaling $300,000.  A loan-servicing company bought house # 3 for $120,000 at the auction. Applicant  testified  that  shortly  after the foreclosure  sale he  received  a  packet  in the  mail  from  an  unknown  loan-servicing  or  collection  agent  at  SOR  ¶  1.d   demanding that he pay approximately $230,000, which presumably was for the balance he  owed  after  foreclosure  on  the  two  mortgage  loans.
