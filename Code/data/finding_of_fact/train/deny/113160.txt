In Applicant’s SOR responses, he admitted the SOR allegations in ¶¶ 1.a through 1.d,  and  he  denied  SOR  ¶¶  1.e  through  1.g.    He  also  provided  extenuating  and mitigating information.  His admissions are accepted as findings of fact. Applicant is a 40-year-old information security consultant who has worked for his current  defense-contractor  employer for four years.    In 1998,  he  graduated from high school, and he attended college; however, he did not receive a degree.   He  has  no  military  service.    He  was  married  from  2002  to  2008,  and  he married again in 2016.  He has two children ages 2 and 14 and three step children  ages  15,  19,  and  21.    He  has  not  had  any  unemployment  in  the previous five years.  He is not required to pay any child support.  Financial Considerations SOR ¶¶ 1.a and 1.b allege Applicant failed to timely file his federal income tax return for tax year 2015 and his state tax return for tax year 2015. Applicant prepared his 2015 federal income tax return; however, he did not mail it to the IRS.  He lost track of his 2015 federal income tax return on his cluttered desk.  His work schedule was demanding and hectic, and he did not give a high-enough priority to getting his 2015 tax returns filed.  He mostly attributed the failure to file his 2015 federal income tax return to an oversight.  According to his 2015 federal income tax return, he owes the  IRS $14,000.    He plans  to file  the  2015 federal income tax  return soon.    He  did  not  timely  file  his  federal  income  tax  returns  for  tax  years  2011 2 through 2018, although several of those years could have been filed within the extension periods.  His state tax returns for those same years were not timely filed.  SOR ¶ 1.c alleges Applicant owes federal income taxes of about $6,000 for tax year 2016. He acknowledged that he owed the tax debt for tax year 2016.  He did not resolve his federal tax debt for five reasons:  procrastination;  busy with heavy demands from work;  family issues;  use of funds for travel; and  payments of 25 percent of his income to address his state income tax debt.  The family issues involved  custody  of  his  daughter  and  taking  his  children  to  sporting  events.    In September 2019, he completed an online payment agreement with the IRS relating to his 2011 to 2016 federal income taxes.  He estimated his federal tax debt to be about $35,000 including tax year 2015.  His first payment of $521 was made to the IRS in September 2019 under his payment plan.  SOR ¶ 1.d alleges Applicant owes state taxes of about $9,000. From 2012 to 2018, the state tax authority filed six tax liens against Applicant.  Applicant had a wage garnishment to collect a $47,000 state tax debt.  All of Applicant’s state income taxes are now paid.  He believed he filed his state income tax return for tax year 2015.  SOR ¶ 1.e alleges Applicant has a past-due vehicle financing debt for $1,255. One of Applicant’s payments was returned for insufficient funds, and the creditor reported the debt as past due.  The debt is now current.  SOR ¶ 1.f alleges Applicant has a charged-off account for $1,285. Applicant rented furniture  in  2010,  and  the  most  recent  payment  was  in  2011.    Applicant contacted  the  creditor,  and  the  creditor  was  unable  to  locate  the  debt.    The account is closed.  Applicant’s current annual base salary is about $200,000 with a potential $30,000 bonus.  Applicant is current on his student loan payments.  He has not had any financial counseling.  Now that the state-wage garnishment is complete, he has the available funds to maintain currency on his debt and to establish and complete an IRS payment plan.  He has been entrusted with sensitive information during his career, and he has conscientiously protected the information.  Two  coworkers  who  have  known  Applicant  for  five  to  seven  years  positively described  Applicant  work performance  and character.  He  is  an  honest,  diligent, professional who makes significant contributions to his employers.
