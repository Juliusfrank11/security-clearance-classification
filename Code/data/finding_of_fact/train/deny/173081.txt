In Applicant’s SOR response, he admitted parts of the SOR allegations in ¶¶ 1.a through 1.c, and he denied other parts of the SOR allegations.  He also provided mitigating information.   His  admissions are  accepted as findings  of fact.  Additional findings follow. Applicant  is  37-year-old  senior  logistician.    In  2006,  he  received  a bachelor’s  degree  with  a  major  in  political  science,  and  in  2013,  he  was  awarded  a master’s degree in data and supply chain management.  He served on active duty for nine years in the Marine Corps, and then continued in the Marine Corps Reserve.  He is currently a major.  In 2006, he married, and his four children are ages six, seven, eight, and ten.  Financial Considerations Applicant’s current  annual salary is $135,000, and  his spouse’s annual salary is $155,000.    He  has  an  additional  annual  income  of  about  $20,000  from  other sources.  He has substantial funds in the bank.  From 2016 to 2022,  Applicant  and  his  spouse’s  gross  annual  income  averaged  from  $250,000  to $300,000.    He  estimated  his  net  worth  to  be  about  $650,000.    His  net monthly remainder is about $8,000.  Several years ago, Applicant spent $42,000 in medical and legal expenses to adopt a child.  The adoption expenses reduced Applicant’s  available  funds.  Applicant’s  spouse  is  a  physician’s  assistant.   2 Applicant and his spouse invested in several real estate investment properties over the years. Around  2014  to  2015,  he  spent  about $180,000 to expand the house from 1,400 square feet to 3,500 square feet.  He contracted with a construction company  and agreed to pay $119,495 to CC to remodel his residence.  He had $80,000 cash, and he borrowed the remainder on credit cards. Applicant paid the final payment to the CC before the work was completed, and then CC left without completion of the work. Applicant  was  unsure about why he did not sue CC; however, it may have been an inability to collect from CC and a reluctance to pay litigation costs when CC might not pay any judgment obtained from a court.  In 2021, Applicant sold the house and made a $130,000 profit after the mortgage was paid.  The  SOR  alleged  Applicant  has  three  charged-off  debts  totaling  $44,471  as follows: SOR ¶¶ 1.a , 1.b , and 1.c . DRC wrote the creditors in SOR ¶¶ 1.a and 1.b; however, DRC said  the  creditors  “did  not  respond  and  chose  not  to  pursue  the  accounts  so  now  the statute of limitations has expired, and they cannot legally collect these debts. We will now be  challenging  the  accounts  on  the  credit  report  to  be  removed.”   DRC advised Applicant not to pay the creditors in SOR ¶¶ 1.a and 1.b. Applicant did  not  indicate   that   DRC   ever offered   a   specific settlement amount  to   the   creditors in SOR ¶¶  1.a  and  1.b.  He had  the  funds available to  pay  the  debt owed  to  SOR ¶   1.b   in   full   if   he   chose   to   do   so.    Applicant  provided   a   February   17,  2022 TransUnion  credit report which did not include  the  delinquent debts in SOR ¶¶  1.a  and 3 1.b; however, his December 7, 2021 Equifax credit report lists the debt in SOR ¶ 1.b, but not the debt in SOR ¶ 1.a.  Applicant said the three-year statute of limitations barred collection of the debts in SOR  ¶¶  1.a  and  1.b,  and  he  believed  the  debt  was  “no  longer  valid.”    The statute of limitations for a credit card debt in the state where his residence was located in 2015 and 2016 is five years. DRC said the creditor in SOR ¶ 1.c “chose not to respond and then charged off the account  and  did  not  pursue  it  despite  our  attempts  and  it  is  now  past  the  statute  of limitations and cannot legally be enforced.”  In 2015, the creditor in SOR ¶ 1.c issued an  IRS  Form  1099-C  indicating  the  creditor  cancelled  a  debt  owed  by  Applicant  for $18,093. Applicant  explained  that  he  did  not  pay  the  debts  in  SOR  ¶¶  1.a,  1.b,  and  1.c because CC should have repaid the funds Applicant borrowed and paid to CC to complete work  that  was  not  performed.    Applicant  wanted  DRC  to  convince  some  of  his creditors to seek repayment from CC rather than from him.  He was “under the impression” from  DRC that  the  creditors could  seek repayment from CC and when the creditors elected not to do so, Applicant elected not to repay the creditors in SOR ¶¶ 1.a and 1.b. Applicant conceded that the lack of privity made enforcement by the creditors unlikely, that is, the contract between Applicant and CC is only binding on Applicant and CC , and creditors did not have a contractual basis to  enforce the contract.  Two  non-SOR  creditors  responded  to  DRC’s  request  for  information,  and  DRC settled both debts on Applicant’s behalf.  Applicant established payment plans for some of his other debts. Aside from the two debts in SOR ¶¶ 1.a and 1.b, Applicant has excellent credit.
