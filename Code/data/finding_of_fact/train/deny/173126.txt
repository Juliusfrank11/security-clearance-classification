In Applicant’s SOR response, he admitted the allegations in SOR ¶¶ 1.a, 1.b, 1.e, 1.f, and  2.b.   He  denied  the other SOR allegations.  He also provided mitigating information.  His admissions are accepted as findings of fact. Applicant is a  39-year-old employee  of a  DOD contractor who  has worked  for this employer since October 2018.  In 2000, he graduated  from high school.   In  2019, he  received  a  bachelor’s degree.    He served  in the  Navy  from 2007  to  August 2016.  He received  a  general discharge  under honorable  conditions.    He  was a  petty   officer second   class    when  he   was discharged.     His Navy   specialty   was  logistics.    He  was  married   from   2008   to   2011,  and   his  two children   are   ages  12   and   14.      His  dating   relationship   with   his  former  spouse continued  until 2013.  Financial Considerations Applicant  cited  a  contentious  divorce  in  2011  as  the  genesis  of  his  financial problems leading up to his 2014 bankruptcy.  He had expensive legal bills because his former spouse made allegations against him.  He went back to family court three or four times to clarify visitation rules.  He was not deployed after 2012.  He was unemployed from August to October 2016, from January to September 2017, and  from  April  to  October  2018.    On  April  16,  2019,  an  Office  of Personnel  Management   investigator  confronted  Applicant  about  several delinquent debts.  His current annual salary is $48,000. His monthly child-support obligation is $1,100, and  he  has  the  child-support  payment  made  automatically  from  his paycheck.  His monthly rent is $1,250, and his car payment is $600.  He said he uses 2 a written budget, and he said he would provide it after his hearing.  However, he did not provide his budget.  His tax returns are filed, and he does not currently owe taxes. Criminal Conduct The SOR contains the following criminal conduct allegations: SOR ¶  2.a  alleges Applicant received nonjudicial punishment   while in the Navy  for  falsifying  dependency  documentation  in  order  to  receive  additional  financial benefits. In his February 28, 2019 SCA Applicant said he received NJP in October 2014 for “failure to obey an order [and] false statements.”  He was “found guilty,” and he received 45 days of extra duty.  He said his command learned he was not married when his former spouse called his command and informed them of the change in  his marital status.    Applicant  admitted  that he failed  to  update  his personnel records  to  reflect  his  divorce.    Applicant  told  an  OPM  investigator  that  the falsification related to an allegation he was claiming “additional funds on his taxes.”  He said it was an oversight, and he did not receive any additional benefits.  In  his  follow-up  September  17,  2019  OPM  interview,  Applicant  said  his  former spouse was listed as his dependent on his military dependency document, which would have  incorrectly  entitled  her  to  military/TRICARE  medical  benefits,  use  of  the commissary, possession of a Navy identification card, etc.  He received 45 days of extra duty, reduction to E-4 , and forfeiture of $1,213 pay per month for two months  from his commander as a result of the NJP disposition.  There is no record evidence that Applicant received more pay due to his records incorrectly indicating he was married in 2014 when he was actually divorced in 2011. There is no evidence his former spouse fraudulently received medical care or other  benefits  to  which  she  was  not  entitled  due  to  his  failure  to  timely  update  his personnel records concerning his divorce.
