In  Applicant’s SOR response, he  admitted  the  SOR allegations  in ¶¶  1.a  through 1.d.     He  also  provided   mitigating   information.  His   admissions   are   accepted   as findings  of  fact. Additional findings follow. Applicant  is  a  74-year-old  electronics  engineer,  who  has  worked  for  the  same defense contractor since 1985.  He has 37 years of experience as an electronics engineer.  He honorably served on active duty in the Army from 1969 to 1971,  and  in  the  Army  Reserve  from  1971  to  1975.    He  did  not  serve  in  a combat  zone.   When  he  left  the  Army he  was  a  Specialist 5  .   He received a bachelor’s degree in 1972.  He married the first time in 1969, and he divorced in 1984.  He married the second time in 1986, and he divorced in 2003.    He  does  not  have  any  children.    He  has  held  a  security clearance for 37 years as a contractor and during his time in the Army.  There is  no  evidence  of  security  violations.  His  resume  provides  additional  details  about  his professional background.    He  needs his  security clearance to  retain  his current employment.  Financial Considerations Applicant’s  annual  base  pay  is  about  $200,000,  and  he  sometimes  receives  a bonus of about $12,000.  He also receives about $45,000 annually from the Social Security Administration .  He started receiving SSA payments when he reached age 70.  Applicant  had  a  girlfriend,  and  her  daughter  was  born  in  1998.    His girlfriend was addicted to the use of illegal drugs.  Applicant provided money 2 to his girlfriend for living expenses, and she said she had to pay her drug dealer, who was threatening her life.  She showed him a broken window and a bullet hole to convince him the threats were real.  He did not indicate that he called the police or that he ensured his payments were not used to procure illegal drugs. In 2014 and 2015, he took extra funds out of his 401 account to assist his girlfriend.  He was unsure about how much he provided to her.  He did not intend for her to use the money he gave her to purchase illegal drugs.  He reduced his 401 account from about $300,000 to about $50,000.  He temporarily stopped paying his debts and filing his federal income tax returns.  He conceded, “when you’re in love, you do stupid things, I guess.”  Applicant’s girlfriend died of a heart attack shortly after leaving a drug-treatment program.  He continues to provide financial support to his girlfriend’s daughter, who is now 23 years old.  The debts in SOR ¶¶ 1.b , 1.d , 1.e , and 1.f  became delinquent in 2015 and 2016.  In December 2019, Applicant paid a debt- settlement company  a $1,500 retainer because he had difficulty determining which creditor  held  these  four  debts.    He  asked  DSC  to  attempt  to  reach settlement  agreements  for  these four  debts on  his behalf.    In  December 2019, DSC sent letters to the four creditors indicating he had a significantly reduced income; he was  considering  filing  for  bankruptcy;  and  payment  was  “infeasible.”   Applicant did not review the letters DSC sent to his creditors before they were sent.   Applicant  suggested  the  language  of  the  letters  to  the  creditors  was  DSC attempting to negotiate a favorable settlement. The  creditors  did  not  reply to  DSC’s settlement offers.  DSC said the debt might be collection barred due to the statute  of  limitations. DSC recommended  that he  not  contact his creditors because if he “appeared too eager, they would not negotiate very well.”  Applicant did not intend to directly contact the SOR creditors.  If DSC was unable to make progress resolving his debts, he may consider hiring a different lawyer to assist him.  On  April  24,  2020,  Applicant  told  an  Office  of  Personnel  Management   investigator that he hoped to get a payment plan in place for each of his creditors, and he had sufficient income and an inheritance which would enable him to pay his debts.
