In  Applicant’s  SOR  response,  he  partially  admitted  and  partially  denied  the allegations in SOR ¶¶ 1.a through 1.f.  He also provided mitigating information.  His admissions are accepted as findings of fact. Applicant is a 64-year-old telecommunications technician who has worked for DOD contractors at the same location since 1985.  He is a high  school  graduate,  and  in  2005,  he  received  an  associate’s  degree  in  electronics technology.  He was married from 1985 to 2005, and his sons are ages 29 and 31.  He was deployed to Germany while he was on active duty in the Air Force.  He received an Outstanding Unit Award and an Air Force Commendation Medal.  He honorably served in the Air Force from 1978 to 1984.   He has held a security clearance for about 40 years.  There is no evidence of security violations, arrests, convictions, use of illegal drugs, or abuse of alcohol.  Financial Considerations In the last 10 years, Applicant’s income has varied from $60,000 to $80,000.   His base  salary  is  $44  an  hour, and  he  frequently  receives overtime  pay.  His total monthly   expenses   are   about   $1,750.     He   has   a   monthly   remainder  of  about $2,000.  His adjusted  gross income   on  his IRS  Form  1040  for three  recent tax   years    was  as  follows  :  2019   ;  2020 ;  and  2021  .  Each  TY  he  owed  $500  to  $1,000.    Thus, no federal income  tax  refund  was available for transfer to  address unpaid  federal student loans  for those three  TYs. He does not owe any tax debts. Applicant has about $24,000 in his checking account, a certificate of deposit valued at $104,000, and about $885,000 in a retirement account.  After his divorce, 2 he was supposed to receive about $700 monthly from his former spouse because he had custody of his two sons.  The confluence of events relating to his divorce, decline in the housing market, and his sons needing financial support while in college caused his financial problems.  He elected to financially support his sons instead of paying his mortgage.  His two sons currently have excellent jobs.  The January 28, 2021 SOR alleges seven delinquent debts as follows: SOR  ¶  1.a  alleges  a  $500  delinquent  medical  debt,  which  resulted  when Applicant’s  son  was  injured  and  taken  to  the  hospital  in  an  ambulance.    He believed his son requested that his health insurance cover the bill.  His son moved five times, and his son evidently did not receive the bills.  Several months before his hearing he contacted his son, and his son said he would take care of the bill.  On July 19, 2022, Applicant paid this debt, and he provided a confirmation number.  SOR  ¶¶  1.b  through  1.e  allege  four  student-loan  debts  totaling  about  $48,600 placed for collection for $8,360, $18,215, $13,666, and $8,352. Applicant borrowed the funds to enable his son to attend college.  His son graduated from college in 2019, and then his student loans were deferred for six months.  Applicant received delinquency notices and contacted his son. He erroneously believed his son was paying on the loans because his son repeatedly assured him he was paying the student loans; however, his son was apparently making payments on different student loans.  After  receiving  two  or  three  notices,  around  March  2020,  Applicant’s  pay  was garnished  for  $700  a  month.    After  his  pay  was  garnished,  he  called  the Department of Education  and asked them to transfer the loans under his son’s name; however, D. Ed. refused.  Applicant’s son agreed to pay Applicant, and then Applicant was going to pay D. Ed.  Garnishments occurred in March and April 2020.  In March 2020, as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic, the D. Ed. placed federal student loans in forbearance, and Applicant’s scheduled garnishment for May 2020 did not  occur.    The  D.  Ed.  extended  the  student-loan  payment  pause  through August 31, 2022. The pause includes the following relief measures for eligible loans: a suspension of loan payments; a 0% interest rate; and stopped collections on defaulted loans. SOR ¶¶  1.f  and  1.g  allege  a  delinquent mortgage  and a home  equity  account owed to  the  same mortgage company  which may  have  been  resolved  through  a foreclosure in 2015. In  2003, Applicant purchased  a  home  in 2003  with  a  $140,000  mortgage, which was  subsequently   refinanced   for  $165,000.    In   2008,  he   borrowed   $95,000 through  a  home  equity  loan.    In  2015,  he  was not  receiving  all  of the  $700  monthly payments  from his former spouse  for child  support, and  his two  sons were in college  and had  significant expenses.  He could  not afford the  payments on  his mortgage and  home  equity  loan.  He said “the  financial demands of  my  two  kids [were] more important  [than]  saving   the   family   home.”    He  discussed   refinancing   with   his mortgage  company, and  they  may  have  suggested  that  he  stop  making  payments.   He  kept  providing  documentation  for the  refinancing  of  his residence;  however, the mortgage  company  was never satisfied  with  the  documentation  he  submitted.  He  did   not  make   any   payments   for  6   to   12   months   and   the   creditor foreclosed   on   the property in 2015.   Applicant learned that the federal government had sued  his mortgage lender for unfair lending practices.  He contacted the federal government; however, he was not provided documentation showing he was added as a plaintiff to the lawsuit.  He did not attempt to sell his house as a short sale.  He was unaware of the amount  the house was  sold for at  the foreclosure.   He was  not  specifically advised whether there was a deficiency as a result of the foreclosure.  Applicant’s  May  2,  2022  credit  report  indicates  a  high  credit  $140,000  joint mortgage  account,  “pays  account  as  agreed”  status,  actual  payment  $68,847,  and  a current  balance  of  zero.    His  November  25,  2017  credit  report  indicates  a $95,000 debt owed to the same creditor as his mortgage.  The $95,000 debt is shown with an account closed designation, foreclosure, and a zero current balance.   Court  documents  indicate  the  foreclosure  amount  was  $169,712.    In March  2016,  the  court  disbursed  a  “foreclosure  surplus.”    I  requested  that Applicant find out whether there was a deficiency and the status of the foreclosure after the hearing.  However, in light of the evidence of record indicating resolution through forfeiture seven years ago in 2015, I am satisfied that the two debts related to his residence are resolved.
