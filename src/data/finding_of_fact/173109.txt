In  Applicant’s SOR response, he  admitted  the  SOR allegations  in ¶¶  1.a  through
1.d. (HE  3) His  admissions  are  accepted as  findings  of  fact. Additional findings follow.
Applicant is a 55-year-old project manager, and he has worked for the contractor
in this role for four or five years. (Tr. 6, 8; GE 1) In 1986, he graduated from high school.
(Tr. 6) He attended college and has almost completed his associate’s degree. (Tr. 7, 17)
He served in the Navy from 1986 to 2007, and he honorably retired as a chief petty officer
(E-7).  (Tr. 7,  18)  His  Navy  specialty  was  electrician’s mate.  (Tr. 7)  Applicant has  a 60
percent disability rating from the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA). (Tr. 33)
There  is no  evidence that  Applicant  uses  illegal drugs,  has  engaged  in  criminal
conduct, violated security, or has issues with excessive alcohol consumption. (GE 1) He
has  held  a  security  clearance  for  more  than  30  years.  (Tr.  19)  He  is  an  outstanding
member of his community. (Tr. 41)
In 1986, Applicant married, and his three children are ages 32, 34, and 35. (Tr. 8)
His spouse is not employed outside their home. (Tr. 30) One of his children, her husband,
and two of his grandchildren live with Applicant. (Tr. 9) His daughter and son-in-law are
employed and pay Applicant $400 monthly for household expenses. (Tr. 32)
Financial Considerations
SOR ¶¶ 1.a and 1.c allege Applicant failed to file his federal and state income tax
returns for tax year (TY) 2016. SOR ¶¶ 1.b and 1.d allege Applicant failed to file his federal
and state income tax returns for TY 2017.
When Applicant completed his November 27, 2017 SCA he disclosed that he had
not filed his federal and state income tax returns for TY 2016, and he indicated he did not
file his tax returns because he “couldn’t afford to pay” his taxes. (GE 1 at 35) He estimated
that  he  owed  $1,500,  and  he  believed  he  would  have  the  necessary  funds  when  he
received a settlement from an insurance company. (Id.)
On  December  26,  2019,  Applicant  responded  to  DOHA  interrogatories,  and  he
disclosed that he had not filed his federal and state tax returns for TYs 2016 and 2017.
(GE 2) He estimated that he owed about $3,000 to the federal government, and he did
not owe any state taxes. (Id.)
On  February  8, 2021, Applicant responded  to  the  SOR,  and  he  indicated  that he
had  “procrastinated” in  filing  his  federal and  state  tax  returns  for TYs 2016  and  2017. (HE
3) He noted  that he  was in an  automobile  accident in September 2016  which prevented
him  from  filing  his tax  returns,  and  he  was gathering  the  documentation  needed  to  file  his
tax returns. (Id.)
At his hearing, Applicant said he  was out of work for about three  months after the
automobile  accident in  September 2016,  and  he  received  short-term  disability  payments.
(Tr. 23, 26) He  received  $19,000  from  a  lawsuit involving  the  accident. (Tr. 27) Applicant’s
annual income  from  2014  to  2019  was about $100,000  for each  of those  years. (Tr. 24,
33)  He  filed  his  tax  returns when  due  for TYs 2018  through  2020,  and  he  received  refunds.
(Tr. 29; GE 2)
In 2020, Applicant’s home was flooded, and his documentation was damaged or
mixed up when it was collected and moved out of his home in boxes. (Tr. 34) In 2021, his
home had a fire and his papers were mixed up again when they were moved out in boxes.
(Tr.  34-35) He  had  some  deaths  in  his family  that  caused  delay  in  the  filing  of  his tax
returns.  (Tr.  33-36)  He  did  not  specify  who  died,  when  they  died,  or  how  their  deaths
caused him not to file his tax returns for TYs 2016 and 2017.
Around   October  2021,   Applicant   contacted   a   tax   return   preparation   service  for
assistance  (Tr. 28-29)  His tax returns do  not involve itemized  deductions, and  he  has W-
2s showing  his income. (Tr. 34-35) He  has the  funds in  the  bank to  pay  his taxes, and  he
intends to  file his tax returns soon. (Tr. 34-35)
